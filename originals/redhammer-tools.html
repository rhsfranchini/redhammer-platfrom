Sales Philosophy
We Don't Sell. We Solve.

RedHammer Sales Philosophy

Our guiding principles for building trust and solving real problems in construction accounting.

At RedHammer, we believe the best salespeople aren't salespeople at all. They're problem solvers who happen to have a solution worth buying.

Our approach is simple: shut up and listen. The prospect knows their business better than we ever will. Our job is to understand their world deeply enough that when we do speak, what we say actually matters.

The Core Principles

1. Curiosity Over Pitching

We lead with questions, not credentials. The first 20 minutes of any conversation should be dominated by their voice, not ours. We're not waiting for our turn to talk - we're genuinely trying to understand what's broken, what's working, and what keeps them up at night.

When someone tells us about their accounting chaos or their frustration with software that promised everything and delivered nothing, we want to feel that with them. Not because it's a sales tactic, but because we've seen it a hundred times and we know how much it costs them - in money, in time, in stress.

2. Emotion Before Logic

People don't buy accounting services because of features. They buy because they're tired. They're frustrated. They're embarrassed that a $20 million company can't produce accurate financials. They're scared they're going to lose a bond or miss payroll.

We create space for those feelings to surface. We validate them. We let prospects know they're not alone and they're not stupid - they're just dealing with a problem that requires specialized expertise they shouldn't be expected to have.

The logical stuff - scope, pricing, timelines - comes later. First, they need to feel understood.

3. Advisor, Not Vendor

We're not here to sell RedHammer services. We're here to help construction companies figure out what they actually need - and then determine if we're the right ones to provide it.

Sometimes that means recommending software we don't profit from. Sometimes it means telling a prospect that Sage is wrong for them even though we could do the implementation. Sometimes it means saying "you're not ready for us yet" and pointing them somewhere else.

This isn't altruism. It's strategy. When you consistently give people the right answer instead of the profitable answer, they trust you. And trust is the only sustainable competitive advantage in a relationship business.

4. Educate to Differentiate

Most prospects come to us with incomplete information. They've been told by a software vendor that their problems will disappear with a new system. They've been burned by a bookkeeper who claimed to know construction. They've read articles that say QuickBooks can't handle real job costing.

We reframe their thinking. We explain the software gap. We challenge assumptions. We show them possibilities they didn't know existed.

When we're done, they don't just want to hire us - they understand why. That's a client who stays.

5. Transparency Builds Trust

We tell prospects when we're slammed and can't start for six weeks. We give them real pricing ranges instead of dodging the question. We admit when something is outside our expertise.

Construction people have finely tuned BS detectors. They've been lied to by subs, burned by vendors, and overpromised by consultants. The fastest way to stand out is to just tell the truth, even when it's inconvenient.

6. Know When to Stop Selling

When a prospect says "this is exactly what we've been looking for," the conversation is over. Not literally - but the selling is. From that moment forward, we're coordinating next steps, not adding more reasons to buy.

Talking past the close doesn't demonstrate expertise. It demonstrates insecurity. When they're ready, we stop and we move.

What This Looks Like in Practice

A typical RedHammer introduction call runs 30-45 minutes. Here's how that time breaks down:

70%
They're talking. We're asking questions, listening, and validating.
5%
We briefly explain who RedHammer is - just enough context to establish credibility.
20%
We're educating, reframing, and discussing options based on what we've learned.
5%
We're aligning on next steps.

We don't use scripts in the traditional sense. We have a framework - a sequence of phases that guides the conversation - but every call is different because every prospect is different.

The goal isn't to close a deal on the first call. The goal is to deeply understand their situation and determine if there's a real fit. If there is, the deal closes itself.

Why This Works

Construction is a relationship business. These are people who've built careers on handshakes and reputation. They can smell a slick sales pitch from a mile away, and they hate it.

What they respond to is competence and authenticity. Someone who actually knows their industry. Someone who asks smart questions. Someone who doesn't pretend to have all the answers but clearly has more answers than most.

That's what we aim to be in every conversation. Not the pushy vendor trying to hit a quota. The knowledgeable partner who's trying to figure out if we can genuinely help.

When we get that right, we don't have to sell. They ask us how to get started.

The Bottom Line

  • Listen more than you talk.
  • Understand before you prescribe.
  • Tell the truth, even when it's hard.
  • Educate instead of pitch.
  • Stop selling when they're ready to buy.

This approach won't work for everyone. It requires patience, expertise, and a genuine belief that helping people is more important than closing deals.

But for the clients who find us - the ones who've been burned before and are looking for someone they can actually trust - it's exactly what they need.

And those are the only clients we want anyway.

  1. Understand their business and pain ‚Äî Learn revenue, software, job costing practices, team structure, and the emotional driver behind the call
  2. Make them feel heard and validated ‚Äî Let them talk 70% of the time, validate their frustration, summarize what you heard before moving forward

Target: 1-2 minutes

Open with gratitude and curiosity

"Thanks for taking the time. How did you find us?"

Listen and acknowledge their response. This validates marketing efforts and creates an easy entry point.

"Great - tell me about your company. What are you trying to accomplish, and what's getting in the way?"

"I'd love to hear more about your story and what's going on."

Wait for confirmation. Adjust if needed.

Phase 4: Brief RedHammer Introduction

Target: 60-90 seconds

Keep it tight. Hit the key points:

"But really, my job right now is to understand your situation and figure out if we can help."

On team structure (simplified)

"You get a dedicated team with a single US-based point of contact. We divide and conquer behind the scenes but keep communication centralized so you're not juggling multiple people."

Phase 5: Consultative Problem-Solving

Target: 10-15 minutes

Educate. Reframe. Challenge assumptions.

Introduce the software gap concept when relevant

"You're sitting in what I call the software gap. On one side you have QuickBooks - easy to use, flexible, but limited on construction functionality. On the other side you have NetSuite, Intact - powerful but expensive and complex. In the middle you have Sage 100, Sage 300, Foundation - great functionality but terrible technology. No APIs, no bank feeds, no AI. They're going backwards while everything else moves forward."

Challenge their assumptions when appropriate

If they mention... Say...
Sage 300 "You'd be going backwards on technology. Everything that's easy in QuickBooks becomes hard."
Intact for a smaller company "That's way too big for your size. You'll spend six figures implementing something you'll never fully use."
Dismissing QuickBooks "90% of the QuickBooks files we see aren't set up correctly. The software can do it - it's the configuration that's failing."

Introduce alternatives they haven't considered

"Have you looked at IES - Intuit Enterprise Suite? It's QuickBooks on steroids. Budgets, change orders, 11 additional construction reports, AI functionality. Construction edition is coming in February with AIA billing."
"There are also bolt-on options - Knowify for specialty subs, BuilderTrend for GCs, Adaptive for AP automation and job costing. They integrate with QuickBooks and fill the gaps without forcing you into an expensive ERP."

Position yourself as the guide

"We're not in the software selling business. We don't make money recommending one system over another. Our job is to help you find the right solution for where you are now and where you're headed."

Give ballpark pricing when asked (don't defer too long)

"For a company your size, monthly maintenance typically runs $4,000 to $6,000 depending on services and transaction volume. Implementation depends on cleanup - could be $10K if things are in good shape, $20K or more if there's significant remediation. But let me look under the hood first to give you a real number."

Phase 6: Recognize Buying Signals and Transition

Listen for signals they're ready

  • "This is exactly what we've been looking for."
  • "You guys seem like the right fit."
  • "What are the next steps?"
  • "How do we get started?"
When you hear these - stop selling and start coordinating:
"Great. Let's talk about how we move forward."
Do not continue explaining. Transition immediately to next steps.

Phase 7: Next Steps

Target: 3-5 minutes

Clearly articulate the path forward

"Here's how we typically work. I'll send over an NDA today. Once that's signed, you give us accountant access to your system. We take 3-4 days to do an assessment - look at the health of the books, the configuration, how job costing is set up, transaction volume."
"From there, we come back with observations - what's working, what's not, opportunities we see. And a recommended plan."
"Then we can sit down and talk through services - what you want us to handle, what stays with your team - and I'll come back with a real quote."

Be transparent about capacity

"I'll be straight with you - we're busy right now. We have a few assessments ahead of you. But if we get the NDA and access this week, I can have observations back to you by [specific date]."

Set clear expectations on their side

"What I'll need from you is the signed NDA and accountant access. If there's anything unusual about your setup - hosted desktop, VPN required - just let me know and we'll figure it out."

Confirm commitment

Phase 8: Close the Conversation

End with warmth and a clear action:

"Great conversation. I'll follow up today with the NDA and access instructions. Talk soon."

1. Assessment Philosophy

The RedHammer Assessment Mindset

A good assessment is thorough, curious, and strategic about deep dives. You're not just checking boxes‚Äîyou're building a complete picture of how this construction company manages its financial data and identifying gaps between what they're doing and what they should be doing for proper construction accounting.

A typical assessment should take between 60 to 90 minutes. This gives you enough time to be thorough without getting lost in the weeds. If you're finishing in 30 minutes, you're not digging deep enough. If you're going over 2 hours, you're likely getting too detailed in areas that should be noted and moved on from.

Key Principles

  1. Start broad, then drill down ‚Äî Initial observations give you clues about where to focus deep dives
  2. Let the subscription tell the story first ‚Äî A company on QBO Plus can't possibly be doing proper job costing because those reports aren't available
  3. Follow the money ‚Äî Cash, AR, AP, and their reconciliation status tell you about the health of everything else
  4. Construction accounting has specific requirements ‚Äî WIP, job costing, retention, equipment, and indirect cost allocation are non-negotiables
  5. Document everything as you go ‚Äî Narrate your observations; this becomes your report foundation

What Makes Construction Accounting Different

  • Job costing at the cost code level ‚Äî Not just tracking to a project, but to specific cost categories (labor, materials, equipment, subs)
  • WIP (Work in Progress) ‚Äî Over/under billing must be calculated and booked monthly
  • Retention handling ‚Äî Both receivable and payable sides require proper tracking
  • Equipment/fixed assets ‚Äî Heavy equipment companies have dozens of assets requiring proper depreciation
  • Indirect cost allocation ‚Äî Fuel, insurance, small tools need to flow back to jobs
  • Progress billing ‚Äî AIA-style billing with schedule of values
